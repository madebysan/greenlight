# Cast & Crew: Casting Pass

## Casting Thesis
The ensemble must carry the full weight of a film that demands an actor simultaneously sell tax-audit exhaustion (scene 2), kung-fu mastery via fanny-pack nunchucks (scene 17), hot-dog-finger prosthetics as a vessel for tear-wiping empathy (scene 107), and rock-text nihilist philosophy (scene 94)—all without letting the audience feel the tonal seams. Every principal must be able to play multiple character variants across universes while maintaining a single emotional spine: the messy love of a fractured family. The performance burden is not just physical or comedic; it is the actor's job to make the audience believe that a googly eye stuck on a forehead (scene 110) is a legitimate emotional climax.

---

## Principal Roles

### EVELYN WANG
**Casting Energy:** An actor who can hold exhaustion as a baseline state and then snap into explosive physical comedy and martial precision. Must look like someone who hasn't slept in a decade but can suddenly execute sign-spinning choreography (scene 80) and opera singing without irony.

**Performance Burden:** Highest in the cast. Evelyn appears in 27 scenes across 125 pages, with 15 wardrobe changes and requirements for martial arts, sign spinning, opera singing, and hot-dog-finger prosthetics. The actor must convey multiversal mastery while keeping every choice tethered to a woman who is, at her core, a disappointed laundromat owner avoiding her father's judgment (scene 6). The emotional arc demands she be believable as: a woman who introduces her daughter's girlfriend as a "good friend" (scene 6, emotional beat: "Generational shame and frantic evasion"), a rock that has a text-conversation about meaninglessness (scene 94), and a mother who sees her daughter as a bagel-shaped black hole and chooses to pull her out anyway (scene 118).

**Relationship Tension:** Every scene with Joy carries the weight of Johanna's unspoken rejection. Every scene with Waymond carries the weight of divorce papers discovered mid-fight (scene 17). Every scene with Gong Gong carries the weight of a lifetime of never being good enough.

**Scenes That Prove It:**
- Scene 2: Overwhelmed at a table of tax documents, establishing baseline exhaustion
- Scene 48: First autonomous verse jump, screaming "I LOVE YOU" while deflecting an attack in slow motion
- Scene 62: Horror at discovering hot-dog fingers, which must be both absurd and viscerally real
- Scene 94: As a rock, conveying multiversal exhaustion through stillness and text
- Scene 110: Stick googly eye on forehead, turning bullets into harmless eyes—requires joyful, absurd mastery without breaking character

**Physical/Special Demands:** Extensive martial arts choreography (scenes 17, 48, 80, 85, 110), sign spinning (scene 80), opera singing (scene 80), hot-dog-finger prosthetics (scene 62, 107), and the ability to perform while wearing 15 different costumes across tonal shifts.

### WAYMOND WANG / ALPHA WAYMOND
**Casting Energy:** An actor who can be both the sweet, divorce-seeking husband in ill-fitting polo shirts (scene 2, "sweet but seeking a divorce") and the tactical, confident Alpha Waymond who eats a stick of chapstick before a fight (scene 17). The switch must be a surprise, not a gimmick.

**Performance Burden:** Must sell the reveal that the "weak" husband is actually the moral anchor of the multiverse. Alpha Waymond's sacrifice (scene 86, killed by Jobu in the Alphaverse) must land as tragic, not predictable. The actor must make fanny-pack martial arts feel genuinely dangerous, not silly.

**Relationship Tension:** With Evelyn: the quiet ache of a marriage where "Barbie Girl" karaoke (scene 1) is both hollow togetherness and, by scene 118, active loving resistance. With Alpha Evelyn (implied through the shrine in scene 23): the grief of a man who lost his Evelyn and is now training a variant.

**Scenes That Prove It:**
- Scene 14: Opening umbrella to cover security camera, breaking character into Alpha Waymond
- Scene 17: Fanny-pack fight against security guards—comedic and violent simultaneously
- Scene 101: Bleeding from a stab wound, pleading "be kind" - the thematic climax
- Scene 118: Linking arms to pull Joy from the Bagel, transforming from passive to active love

**Physical/Special Demands:** Fanny-pack martial arts choreography (scenes 14, 17, 25), stunts (scene 86 death), and the ability to play two distinct characters who share a body.

### JOY WANG / JOBU TUPAKI
**Casting Energy:** An actor who can play a withdrawn, hoodie-clad daughter who stares blankly into a laundry machine (scene 3) and also an omniversal chaos agent in 20 different costumes who kills by tickling (scene 18). The two must feel like the same person, not a split.

**Performance Burden:** Jobu Tupaki appears in only 10 scenes but requires 20 wardrobe changes—more than any other character. The performance must sell the Bagel philosophy (scene 67) as seductive, not villainous. The final pull from the Bagel (scene 118) requires the actor to convey surrender to nothingness and the desire to be saved simultaneously.

**Relationship Tension:** With Evelyn: the core mother-daughter wound. Every Jobu scene is a cry for Evelyn to see her. The "Duck, Duck, Goose" scene (scene 18) must be terrifying because it's affectionate—Jobu is playing with her victims, not torturing them.

**Scenes That Prove It:**
- Scene 3: Blank stare into laundry machine, anxiety about familial acceptance
- Scene 18: Walking in with small boots, playing Duck Duck Goose before killing
- Scene 56: Stepping out of elevator in Elvis jumpsuit with a pig on a leash
- Scene 67: Opening hands to reveal the Bagel—nihilistic seduction
- Scene 94: As a rock, having a text conversation about meaninglessness
- Scene 118: Being pulled from the Bagel, requiring the actor to convey relief, terror, and love

**Physical/Special Demands:** Stunts, constant rapid wardrobe and makeup changes (20 changes across 10 scenes), pig handler (scene 56), and the ability to do "Duck, Duck, Goose" with the right tone of menace.

### GONG GONG / ALPHA GONG GONG
**Casting Energy:** An actor who can be a hunched, elderly man in slippers (scene 2) and a ruthless military commander in a tricked-out wheelchair with an office-supply robot exoskeleton (scene 67). Must convey generational judgment through posture and silence.

**Performance Burden:** Gong Gong is the source of Evelyn's trauma. His constant criticism (implied through Evelyn's behavior in scenes 2, 6, 12) must be felt even when he's not on screen. Alpha Gong Gong's determination to assassinate his own granddaughter (scene 67) must be understandable as a military necessity, not cartoon villainy.

**Relationship Tension:** With Evelyn: a lifetime of never being enough. With Joy: collateral damage of generational expectations.

**Scenes That Prove It:**
- Scene 2: Sitting in his chair while Evelyn is overwhelmed—silent judgment
- Scene 6: Being introduced to Becky as a "good friend"
- Scene 67: As Alpha Gong Gong, intervening with a makeshift robot exoskeleton
- Scene 118: Linking arms to pull Joy from the Bagel—his only moment of softness

**Physical/Special Demands:** Wheelchair use (scene 2, 6, 12, 118), wheelchair stunts as Alpha Gong Gong (scene 67, 85, 101, 110), and the office-supply robot exoskeleton (scene 67).

### DEIRDRE ATKINS / DEIRDRE (BAGEL)
**Casting Energy:** An actor who can be a frumpy IRS auditor with a hand-grip strengthener (scene 15) and a zealot follower with a sharpie circle on her forehead (scene 18). Must make the mundane feel threatening and the absurd feel bureaucratic.

**Performance Burden:** Deirdre's arc goes from antagonist (scene 15-16) to victim (scene 62, hot-dog universe) to recipient of empathy (scene 107). The actor must sell the pro-wrestling stunts (scene 25), the hot-dog-finger prosthetics (scene 62), and the tear-wiping with toes (scene 107) as a single character.

**Relationship Tension:** With Evelyn: from adversarial audit to mutual absurdity. The hot-dog universe demands they become intimate in a bizarre way.

**Scenes That Prove It:**
- Scene 15: Talking about tax documents while Evelyn switches shoes—mundane menace
- Scene 18: As Bagel Deirdre, ruthless lieutenant
- Scene 62: Hot-dog fingers, absurd terror
- Scene 107: Evelyn wiping her tears with hot-dog toes—radical empathy

**Physical/Special Demands:** Pro-wrestling stunts (scene 25), hot-dog-finger prosthetics (scene 62, 107), stunts as Bagel Deirdre (scene 18, 25, 56, 107).

### BECKY SREGOR
**Casting Energy:** An actor who must be present but not demanding—Becky is Joy's girlfriend, visible only in scenes 3, 4, 6. Must convey patience and support without drawing focus from the Wang family drama.

**Performance Burden:** Becky is the person Evelyn refuses to acknowledge properly (scene 4, "she continually mixes up 'he' and 'she' pronouns"). The actor must make the audience feel the sting of that rejection without overplaying.

**Relationship Tension:** With Joy: a supportive partner who watches her girlfriend be erased. With Evelyn: the object of passive-aggressive deflection.

**Scenes That Prove It:**
- Scene 3: Snaps Joy out of her laundry-machine trance with a kiss
- Scene 6: Being introduced as a "good friend" to Gong Gong

**Physical/Special Demands:** None beyond standard.

## Chemistry Map

### Evelyn & Waymond
The core romantic chemistry must convey decades of quiet disappointment (scene 2, 12, 17 divorce papers) and the rediscovery of why they chose each other (scene 101, 118). Waymond's plea to "be kind" (scene 101) only works if the audience believes Evelyn would listen to this man. The fanny-pack fight (scene 17) works because it shows Waymond as capable in a way Evelyn has never seen—the actor must make that surprising and attractive.

### Evelyn & Joy / Jobu
This is the film's spine. Every Jobu scene is a test: can Evelyn see her daughter? The rock conversation (scene 94) is the purest expression of this—two stones, no distractions. The Bagel pull (scene 118) requires the actors to create a physical tether of love that the audience can feel. The "Duck, Duck, Goose" scene (scene 18) must be terrifying because it's affectionate—Jobu loves her mother and wants her to understand.

### Evelyn & Gong Gong
Generational silence. Gong Gong's judgment is felt in every scene Evelyn apologizes, deflects, or performs. The moment Gong Gong links arms to pull Joy from the Bagel (scene 118) only works if the actor has conveyed stubbornness so complete that this single gesture is monumental.

### Evelyn & Deirdre
The most unlikely pair. The hot-dog universe (scene 62, 107) forces intimacy between two women who would normally never touch. The actor playing Deirdre must make the audience root for her to receive Evelyn's empathy, not just be a punchline.

## Specialty Department Flags

**Stunt Coordinator:** Required for fanny-pack choreography (scene 17, 25), hot-dog-finger fights (scene 62, 107), wheelchair stunts for Alpha Gong Gong (scene 67, 85, 101, 110), pro-wrestling sequences (scene 25), sign-spinning combat (scene 80), and the "Duck, Duck, Goose" death (scene 18).

**Prosthetics/Makeup:** Hot-dog-finger prosthetics for Evelyn and Deirdre (scenes 62, 107). Rapid-change makeup for Jobu Tupaki (20 wardrobe changes across 10 scenes). Googly-eye application (scene 110). The Bagel as a practical effect (scene 67, 91, 118).

**Animal Wrangler:** Pig handler for Jobu's Elvis-jumpsuit entrance (scene 56).

**Dialect Coach:** Evelyn, Waymond, Joy, and Gong Gong are Chinese-American characters. The film mixes English and Chinese in household scenes (scene 1, 2, 4, 6). Consistency is critical for authenticity.

**Prop Master:** The Bagel as a swirling CGI/practical black hole (scene 67, 91, 118). Fanny packs weighted for choreography (scene 14, 17). Googly eyes as craft supplies (scene 6) and as a third eye (scene 110). Tax documents for scene 2. Divorce papers for scene 17. The "Auditor of the Month" trophy (scene 16). Sign-spinning equipment (scene 80). Office-supply robot exoskeleton for Alpha Gong Gong (scene 67).

**Visual Effects Supervisor:** The Bagel (scene 67, 91, 118). Bullets turning into googly eyes (scene 110). Confetti transformation (scene 56). The rock universe (scene 94) requires text-on-screen conversation.

## What Not To Cast

1. **Do not cast an actor for Evelyn who is "funny" first.** The character's comedy comes from exhaustion and desperation, not from comedic timing. Cast someone who can hold stillness and sorrow and let the absurdity break through, not the other way around.

2. **Do not cast an actor for Jobu Tupaki who plays "villain."** Jobu is a child who wants her mother to see the meaninglessness of everything. The character is seductive, not menacing. Cast an actor who can make nihilism feel like a hug.

3. **Do not cast an actor for Waymond who is "strong and silent."** Waymond's power is in his gentleness. Alpha Waymond is competent, but the core Waymond must be someone who could file for divorce sweetly. The fanny-pack fight works because the character is so unexpected.

4. **Do not cast a "character actor" for Deirdre who wants to be scene-stealing.** Deirdre's comedy comes from her bureaucratic earnestness, not from eccentricity. The pro-wrestling stunts and hot-dog fingers must land because she treats them with the same seriousness as an audit.

5. **Do not cast an actor for Gong Gong who is "endearing."** Gong Gong must be intimidating through silence and expectation. The only moment of softness (scene 118) works because it's an earned deviation from a hard character, not a reveal that he was sweet all along.
