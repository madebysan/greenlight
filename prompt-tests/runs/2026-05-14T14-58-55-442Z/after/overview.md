# Overview: Everything Everywhere All At Once

## The Film In One Breath
A Chinese laundromat owner, Evelyn Wang, is audited by the IRS (scene 15) while hiding her daughter Joy's girlfriend from her elderly father Gong Gong (scene 6). Her husband Waymond secretly files for divorce (scene 2). Then Alpha Waymond from the Alphaverse recruits her to stop Jobu Tupaki—a nihilistic chaos entity possessing Joy (scene 18)—who threatens to destroy the multiverse with a bagel-shaped black hole (scene 67). Evelyn learns to verse-jump, fights a fanny-pack duel (scene 17), discovers hot-dog fingers (scene 62), and eventually chooses radical kindness over combat to pull her daughter back from annihilation (scene 118).

## Why This Film
This film promises a theatrical, exhausting ride where every emotional beat lands through absurdist physics and lived-in family dysfunction. The screenplay asks departments to build a world where:

- **A tax audit becomes a kung fu battle.** The IRS 10th floor cubicle (scenes 15-18, 56) transforms from a desk-bound bureaucratic nightmare into a combat zone where Evelyn slips her shoes to the wrong feet (scene 15) to activate a verse jump while being audited.
- **A bagel visualizes nihilism.** The Bagel first appears as a swirling CGI vortex inside Jobu's palms (scene 67), then as a giant practical/CGI black hole behind white veils in Empress Hall (scene 91) that physically sucks characters toward it (scene 118).
- **A fanny pack becomes a weapon.** The fanny pack (scenes 14, 17) must be weighted for choreography but remain a believable suburban-dad accessory, culminating in Alpha Waymond annihilating security guards with it as nunchucks (scene 17).
- **Rock silence speaks louder than noise.** Two rocks on a cliff (scene 94) converse via subtitle about meaninglessness, providing the quietest, most devastating emotional anchor in a film of sensory overload.
- **A pop song transforms meaning.** The family's awkward karaoke rendition of "Barbie Girl" (scene 1) returns as a symbol of active, loving resistance when Evelyn, Waymond, and Gong Gong link arms and sing it to pull Joy from the Bagel (scene 118).

The film asks: "Does this choice make the ridiculous feel emotionally real?" Every prop—from googly eyes stuck on laundry plastic (scene 6) to a baseball bat with a googly eye turned bullets harmless (scene 110)—must serve both a punchline and a gut-punch.

## Scope Read

**Scene Count:** 31 distinct scenes across 18 environment configurations, with nested rapid-cut montages (scenes 14, 18, 90, 118 require hyper-efficient shooting strategies for quick reality-switching).

**Location Pattern:**
- **Primary hub:** IRS building (10 locations across 3 floors: elevator, 8th floor, 9th floor executive office, 9th floor atrium, panic room, stairwell). 16 scenes consolidated in one building.
- **Secondary hub:** Evelyn's apartment dining room/living room (4 scenes: 1, 2, 4, 12) with time variations (NIGHT, MORNING, DAY).
- **Tertiary:** Laundromat (3 scenes: 3, 6, 62), Laundry RV (2 scenes: 13, 23).
- **One-off:** Empress Hall (scene 91), Rock Field EXT (scene 94).
- **Ext/Night:** Only Rock Field (scene 94) is exterior day. No night exteriors.

**Cast Pressure:**
- Evelyn appears in 27 scenes with 15 wardrobe changes and 4 special skills (martial arts, sign spinning, opera singing, hot-dog finger prosthetics).
- Jobu Tupaki appears in 10 scenes with 20 rapid wardrobe/makeup changes and requires a pig handler (scene 56).
- Waymond/Alpha Waymond share 23 scenes combined with fanny-pack choreography.
- Gong Gong requires wheelchair operation in 9 scenes; Alpha Gong Gong requires wheelchair stunts and a makeshift office-supply robot exoskeleton (scene 110).

**VFX/Stunt Burden:**
- **Heavy VFX:** The Bagel (scenes 67, 91, 118)—requires CGI vortex integration with practical lighting interaction and wind machines for Empress Hall (scene 91). Bullet-to-googly-eye transformation (scene 110). Rapid environment switching (scene 90).
- **Stunt sequences:** Fanny-pack martial arts (scenes 14, 17). Pro wrestling moves (scene 25—Deirdre executes backbreaker, drop kick). Luchador pile drive (scene 56). Sign-spinning combat with riot shield (scene 80). Mech-suit battle (scene 110—Alpha Gong Gong's office-supply robot exoskeleton).
- **Prosthetics:** Hot-dog fingers (scenes 62, 107) require practical prosthetics for ketchup/mustard oozing from mouths during love ritual.

**Hidden Complexity:**

1. **The Googly Eye Prop Pipeline.** Googly eyes appear only in scenes 6 (on laundry plastic) and 110 (on baseball bat and Evelyn's forehead)—but the prop must be sourced in a consistent size and adhesive quality for 20+ hero units. The forehead application must survive combat choreography. Bulk redundancy essential.

2. **The IRS Building as One Fluid Set.** The building spans 4 floors (elevator, 8th, 9th, 10th) plus a janitor's closet, panic room, and stairwell. The 9th floor alone has 3 distinct spaces (executive office, atrium, panic room) used across 8 scenes. The entire building functions as one continuous battleground—plan for modular set walls and quick turnovers between floors. The split-screen in scene 16 requires simultaneous Janitor's Closet and Cubicle builds.

3. **Jobu's 20 Wardrobe Changes.** Jobu appears in 10 scenes but requires 20 costume changes—most happen off-screen between reality jumps (scene 90 cycles through Viking, cop/inmate, piñata in minutes). This demands a dedicated wardrobe station with 3-4 dressers on standby for rapid-change moments. The pig in scene 56 requires animal handler clearance and hygiene protocols.

4. **Rock Field Scene 94 Silent Performance.** Two rocks, no dialogue, no movement, subtitled text. This is the emotional breather and thematic fulcrum. The rocks must be practical hero props (not VFX) with subtle texture to read on camera. The field requires a cliff-edge set piece for the "rolling off cliff" beat. This is a full-day shoot for a silent scene that cannot be rushed.

5. **The Apartment Clutter Backlog.** Evelyn's apartment dining room/living room (scenes 1, 2, 4, 12) is described as "a still life of chaos" with workout equipment, self-help books, security feed monitors, rice cooker, microwave, old TV. Every surface must be dressed with intentional clutter that reads on camera. This set must be pre-rigged for quick transitions between NIGHT (scene 1), MORNING (scene 2), and DAY (scenes 4, 12) lighting states. Document every prop placement for continuity across the four scenes.

## Demo-Worthy Hook
The film's final pull (scene 118) requires Evelyn, Waymond, and Gong Gong to physically link arms and sing "Barbie Girl" into a karaoke microphone while being sucked into a bagel-shaped black hole. This is not a joke—it is the emotional climax of a mother accepting her daughter's nihilism and choosing to tether her to life. Every department must build toward this moment: the wind machines pulling hair and fabric, the practical Bagel lighting interacting with the actors' faces, the sound design transitioning from deafening chaos to a pop song sung in harmony. The googly eye on Evelyn's forehead (scene 110) transforms bullets into harmless eyes—a VFX shot that requires tangible prop construction for actor interaction. This is the shot that sells the thesis: absurdity is the only sincere response to the void.
