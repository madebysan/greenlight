# Locations: Scout Pass

## Scout Thesis
This film's locations are not backdrops—they are psychological states. Every space must feel both oppressively real and primed to rupture into the absurd. The IRS building, the apartment, and the laundromat are the three legs of a stool supporting the entire multiverse. The IRS building does triple duty as mundane hell, martial-arts arena, and the stage for a nihilistic black hole. The apartment is the visual equivalent of Evelyn’s cluttered, drowning mind. The laundromat is the family business—the place where generational labor, shame, and love all get tumbled together. These three locations must be buildable, modifiable, and stunt-ready because they will be destroyed, rebuilt, and recontextualized across multiple universes.

---

## Location System

**THE THREE ANCHORS (Hub Locations)** – appear in multiple universes, require buildable sets  
- Evelyn’s Apartment Dining Room/Living Room (scenes 1, 2, 4, 12)  
- Laundromat (scenes 3, 6, 62)  
- IRS Building (scenes 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 25, 56, 67, 80, 85, 86, 101, 110, 124)

**THE VEHICLE** – a moving set piece that changes function between universes  
- Laundry RV (scenes 13, 23)

**THE COSMIC STAGES** – single-use, high-visual-impact spaces  
- Empress Hall (scene 91)  
- Rock Field (scene 94)

**THE SATELLITE SPACES** – small, practical, but crucial  
- Janitor’s Closet (scene 16)  
- IRS Stairwell (scene 48)  
- IRS Elevator (scene 14)  
- IRS Panic Room (scene 80)

## Priority Scout Briefs

### 1. IRIS BUILDING — 10th Floor Cubicle / 8th Floor / 9th Floor Executive Office & Atrium
**Story Job:** The primary battleground. This banal bureaucracy must become a kung-fu arena (scene 17), a pro-wrestling ring (scene 25), a hostage crisis zone (scene 18), a tear-gas siege (scene 80), a mech-suit collision (scene 110), and the site of a multiversal black hole (scene 67, 91). It is the place where Evelyn’s tax problems become existence problems.

**Visual Personality:** Soul-crushing corporate beige with one jarring note of personality—the "Auditor of the Month" trophy (scene 16). Fluorescent lighting. Carpets that smell like stale coffee and photocopier toner. Every surface covered in manila folders and Post-it notes. This must be *the most boring place in the world* so that when Jobu Tupaki walks in with an Elvis jumpsuit and a pig on a leash (scene 56), the contrast is physically jarring.

**Practical Needs:**
- At least two floors of contiguous rental or stage build (10th and 8th at minimum; 9th ideally same building)
- Working elevator with practical doors that can hold a security camera rig (scene 14)
- Breakaway cubicle walls for the fanny-pack fight (scene 17) — these must be light enough for stunt performers to crash through but look like real office partitions
- Breakaway exit sign (scene 25)
- Banisters in the atrium capable of being crushed by a mech suit (scene 110)
- A secret bookshelf door leading to a panic room (scene 80)
- HVAC vents for tear-gas practical effects (scene 80)
- Large open atrium space (scene 86, 101, 110) with enough square footage for choreographed large-scale battles and a mech-suit practical build
- The 9th floor hallway must accommodate a wheelchair crash-through (scene 67)

**Scenes:** 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 25, 56, 67, 80, 85, 86, 101, 110, 124

**Risk:**
- **Stunt density:** This location hosts more action than any other. The fanny-pack fight (scene 17), the Deirdre pro-wrestling (scene 25), the pinky-flick wirework (scene 85), and the mech-suit atrium battle (scene 110) all require different rigging points and floor protection. Expect heavy choreography rehearsal needs.
- **Breakaway volume:** Every fight scene destroys furniture. Scene 17 requires cubicle walls, scene 25 requires a table flip, scene 110 requires banister damage. Budget for daily set restoration.
- **Fire code:** The tear-gas practical effect (scene 80) will require ventilation protocols and possibly hazmat consultation. The panic room is small and enclosed—gas dispersal must be carefully controlled.
- **Camera constraints:** The split-screen closet scene (scene 16) requires simultaneous shooting in two adjacent spaces. The cubicle and closet must be close enough for split-screen logistics.
- **Noise:** Construction for breakaway setups will impact adjacent scenes. Plan a rehearsal block followed by a destruction block.

### 2. EVELYN’S APARTMENT — Dining Room / Living Room
**Story Job:** The emotional baseline. This is where the film opens (scene 1) with a karaoke moment of fragile happiness, and where it closes (implicitly, as the resolution leads back to domestic peace). It must feel like a domestic prison built from objects. Every item visible in scenes 2 and 4 is a weight on Evelyn’s shoulders. The clutter is not decoration—it’s a visual representation of her fractured psyche scraping against the ceiling.

**Visual Personality:** "A still life of chaos" (per script). Workout equipment crammed next to self-help books next to an old TV showing security feed monitors next to a rice cooker next to a microwave (scenes 1, 2, 4, 12). Nothing matches. Nothing is put away. The security feed monitors must be functional or convincingly simulated—they show the laundromat and create a sense of constant surveillance.

**Practical Needs:**
- A practical microwave and rice cooker (scenes 2, 4) — these must be operated by actors
- Security feed monitors with pre-recorded loop or live feed from the laundromat set
- A table large enough to be covered in tax documents and receipts (scene 2) — must look like years of accumulated paperwork
- A mirror that can be shot through for the karaoke opening (scene 1) — hazy, slightly distorting
- Enough clearance for camera movement through the clutter (tight quarters)
- Practical lighting that can shift from warm karaoke glow (scene 1) to harsh morning light (scene 2)

**Scenes:** 1, 2, 4, 12

**Risk:**
- **Continuity nightmare:** The clutter must be documented frame-by-frame. If anything moves between scenes, the "drowning in objects" effect breaks. Hire a dedicated continuity photographer for this set.
- **Time-of-day variations:** Scenes 1 (NIGHT), 2 (MORNING), 4 (DAY), 12 (DAY) — require controllable window light or blackout capability
- **Proximity to laundromat:** The apartment and laundromat are presented as connected (Evelyn thumps the vent with a baseball bat in scene 6 to call Waymond). Ideally, these should be on the same soundstage or at least within walking distance for sound continuity.
- **The karaoke moment (scene 1):** Requires audio playback equipment that doesn’t appear in frame. The TV/monitor showing the karaoke track must be synced with on-set playback.

### 3. LAUNDROMAT
**Story Job:** The family business. It is where Evelyn hits the vent with a baseball bat (scene 6) in frustration, where Joy stares into a tumbling machine (scene 3) and feels the weight of not being accepted, and where the Hot Dog Universe version (scene 62) introduces the film’s most absurd body horror. This space must smell like detergent and feel like work. It is the site of literal labor and emotional labor.

**Visual Personality:** Functional, slightly dingy, but not neglected. Laundry carts, an old arcade cabinet, a TV playing a musical (scene 3, 6), signs for wash & fold. The windows look out onto a street (needs breakaway glass for scene 6). The machines must be real enough to tumble clothes. The arcade cabinet must be authentic-looking or a practical prop.

**Practical Needs:**
- Functional tumbling laundry machines (scenes 3, 6) — must be load-rated if actors lean on them
- Breakaway front window glass (scene 6) — the baseball bat thumping the vent is the first act of physical aggression
- Arcade cabinet (scene 3) — preferably a period-appropriate unit that can be turned on/off
- Ceiling clearance for a vent that can be hit with a bat (scene 6)
- Hot Dog Universe version (scene 62) requires the same set dressed differently or a second build—consider modular dressing so the same space can be re-dressed quickly

**Scenes:** 3, 6, 62

**Risk:**
- **Water/plumbing:** Functional washing machines mean on-set water management. Flooding risk. Need a drain solution and a plumber on standby.
- **Breakaway window continuity:** Scene 6 requires the glass to break. That same window cannot appear fixed in later scenes unless the timeline is carefully managed. Shoot all non-breakaway scenes first.
- **TV playing a musical (scene 3, 6):** Rights clearance for the musical playing. If shooting practical, the screen image must be licensable.
- **Hot Dog Universe dress (scene 62):** Full prosthetic/VFX shoot for the hot dog fingers. The laundromat must accommodate a significant makeup team and VFX reference work. Lighting must be consistent with the other laundromat scenes or differentiated enough to signal a universe shift.

### 4. LAUNDRY RV
**Story Job:** A transitional space that changes function between universes. In the base universe (scene 13), it is a cramped, converted RV filled with racks of clothing and bags of laundry—the mobile extension of the laundromat. In the Alphaverse (scene 23), it is rigged with high-tech wires, screens, and a shrine to Alpha Evelyn. This vehicle must read as both a working-class van and a command center.

**Visual Personality:** Base universe: Cluttered, domestic, slightly shabby. Alphaverse: The same physical space, but now with monitor arrays, cabling everywhere, and an urn/shrine. The contrast should be shocking—same walls, different function.

**Practical Needs:**
- Driving rig or towing capability (scene 13) — Evelyn drives it to the IRS
- Extensive tech cabling and monitor arrays for Alphaverse scenes (scene 23)
- An urn and shrine dedicated to Alpha Evelyn (scene 23)
- A chair rigged with wires for Alpha Waymond (scene 23)
- Enough space for a two-person camera crew and the actors

**Scenes:** 13, 23

**Risk:**
- **Driving shots:** Scene 13 requires the RV to be driven. Options: Process trailer with greenscreen, or a practical driving sequence on a closed road. The RV interior will shake—sound will need ADR for any driving dialogue.
- **Transformation time:** The switch from base-RV to Alphaverse-RV requires set dressing changes. If shooting both in the same day, estimate at least 2 hours for re-dress.
- **Power draw:** The Alphaverse tech rig (scene 23) will require significant electrical load. The RV’s onboard generator may not be sufficient—plan for external power cables.
- **Cramped conditions:** This is a tight interior. Camera placement will be limited. Consider a removable wall panel or a second build for the tech-heavy scenes.

### 5. ROCK FIELD
**Story Job:** The quietest, most emotionally devastating location in the film. Two rocks on a cliff edge, conversing through text (scene 94). This is the breather scene—the moment the film stops screaming and asks: "Does any of this matter?" It must feel infinite, empty, and peaceful. The contrast with the IRS atrium chaos (scene 86, 101, 110) must be total.

**Visual Personality:** Empty dirt field, windswept, with a cliff edge dropping into nothing. Two prominent rocks. No trees, no buildings, no signs of human life. Color palette: brown, gray, sky. The light should be hazy, almost sterile.

**Practical Needs:**
- Two rocks large enough to read as the "characters" — they must be positioned with clear sightlines for the camera
- A cliff edge set piece (safety-rated) — the drop must look significant but be safe for cast/crew
- Wind machines for consistent breeze (the scene needs environmental movement)
- Quiet location (this is a subtitle-only scene; any ambient noise will ruin the silence)
- Access for VFX reference photography (the rocks may be practical, but the background may be CG)

**Scenes:** 94

**Risk:**
- **Weather dependency:** If shooting on location, this is fully exterior. Cloud cover, rain, or wind changes will break continuity. Have a backup interior build option or VFX replacement plan for the background.
- **Cliff safety:** The cliff edge is a set piece. It must be engineered for actor safety and load-tested. No real cliffs—build a practical edge with a safety net or greenscreen drop.
- **Silent shooting:** The scene has no dialogue. Set noise (generators, crew chatter, wind) will be very apparent. Plan for long takes with no interruption.
- **Single-use:** This location appears only once (scene 94). Consider whether it should be a location or a stage build with VFX background.

### 6. EMPRESS HALL
**Story Job:** The visual apex of Jobu Tupaki’s power and nihilism. A grand white hall with massive white veils, behind which swirls the Bagel (scene 91)—a CGI/vortex black hole that represents total entropy. This is where Jobu tries to pull Joy into the void. It must feel sacred, cold, and terrifying.

**Visual Personality:** White on white on white. The hall is grand, almost temple-like. The veils are floor-to-ceiling, moving in wind from the Bagel’s pull. The only non-white element is the swirling dark hole at the center.

**Practical Needs:**
- Massive white veils (scene 91) — must be rigged to drop, billow, and tear
- Wind machines capable of moving the veils and creating a tornado effect
- Enough clearance for the Bagel practical/VFX element — if practical, a large circular set piece; if CG, tracking markers and consistent lighting
- A clean, white floor that can be walked on by multiple actors
- Space for a wide shot showing the scale of the hall vs. the small figures of Evelyn and Jobu

**Scenes:** 91

**Risk:**
- **The Bagel will be a major VFX element (scene 91).** The practical set must support VFX integration: tracking markers, reference lighting, and a greenscreen element for the Bagel’s pull effect.
- **White set challenges:** White shows every scuff, footprint, and dirt mark. The floor will need constant cleaning between takes. Costume color will pop against white—make sure Jobu’s alien-royalty costume (scene 91) doesn’t blend in.
- **Veil rigging:** The veils must be quick-release for safety. They will be pulled, torn, and moved by wind machines. Have backup veils for multiple takes.
- **Single-use:** This scene is the first climax (scene 91). The hall is not seen again. Consider a stage build rather than a location rental.

## Company Moves & Friction

**Biggest pain point: The IRS building is a hub location for scenes 14-18, 25, 56, 67, 80, 85, 86, 101, 110, and 124.** This means the cast and crew will be here for the majority of the shoot. However, the different floors (10th, 8th, 9th) require different dressing. **Scenes 17 and 18 occur on the same floor (10th) but are tonally opposite** — one is a fanny-pack fight, one is a hostage execution. This will require a full set re-dress between takes if shooting sequentially.

**The laundromat and apartment are narratively adjacent (scene 6 connects them via a vent thump).** If these are on different stages or separate locations, you lose the ability to shoot that moment practically. **Ideal scenario: Both on the same soundstage, within 50 feet of each other.**

**The laundry RV (scenes 13, 23) is a driving vehicle that must also be a wired tech set.** This means either a process trailer for driving shots (scene 13) and a stage build for the tech-heavy scenes (scene 23), or a single build that can be towed and then re-dressed. **Expect a 2-3 hour turnaround between the base RV and Alphaverse RV setups.**

**Scenes 90 (Various Universes) and 118 (The Pull) are rapid-cut montages across 10+ universes.** These will require shooting at multiple locations/stages in a single day. Plan for quick turnaround between environments—some may be greenscreen stages with minimal set dressing.

**The rock field (scene 94) is exterior.** If weather is uncooperative, this scene can be moved to a soundstage with a practical rock set and VFX background, but the cost and schedule impact should be weighed against the risk.

**The Empress Hall (scene 91) is single-use but requires significant rigging (veils, wind machines, Bagel integration).** If it’s a location rental, you are paying for a full day for one scene. Consider a stage build.

## Exterior / Night / Crowd Risk

**Night shoots:** Only one night scene — scene 1 (apartment dining room). This can be day-for-night with blackout curtains or shot practical night. Low risk.

**Exteriors:** Only the rock field (scene 94) is definitively exterior. The laundromat windows (scene 3, 6) imply an exterior view, but the action is interior. The IRS building lobby/elevator (scene 14) is interior. **Total exterior time: one day (rock field).**

**Crowd/background:** Scenes set in the IRS building (scenes 15-18, 56) imply office workers, but the script focuses on the main characters. The 10th floor cubicle scene (scene 15-18) may need background actors for the audit session (scene 15) and the aftermath (scene 18). **Estimate 10-15 background actors for the cubicle scenes.** No large crowd scenes.

**Stunt risk (from schedule perspective):** The fanny-pack fight (scene 17), the pro-wrestling (scene 25), the pinky-flick wirework (scene 85), and the mech-suit battle (scene 110) all require dedicated stunt days. **The IRS atrium (scenes 86, 101, 110) is the highest-risk shooting environment** — it hosts the largest-scale action and will require multiple stunt coordinators, safety rigging, and possibly a safety officer dedicated to that space.

**Noise risk:** The laundromat (scenes 3, 6, 62) will have functional washing machines, which create ambient noise. Dialogue in these scenes will require careful mic placement or ADR.

## What To Preserve

1. **The IRS building must feel like a real, working office building, not a set.** The "Auditor of the Month" trophy (scene 16), the cubicle clutter (scene 15), the bulletin boards (scene 25), and the general beige desperation must read as authentic. Do not compromise on this banality—it is the foundation for every multiversal escalation.

2. **The apartment clutter must be a character, not background dressing.** The workout equipment, self-help books, security monitors, rice cooker, and microwave (scenes 1, 2, 4, 12) are visual manifestations of Evelyn’s mental state. Do not simplify or stylize this chaos. It must feel like a real family living in a too-small space with too many objects.

3. **The laundromat must have functional, tumbling machines (scene 3).** Joy staring into the tumbling dryer is her establishing shot — it needs to be a real machine that creates real movement and sound. A static prop will kill the scene.

4. **The rock field (scene 94) must feel infinite and empty.** This is the only moment of peace in the film. Do not compromise on the isolation of this location. A single tree, a road, or a power line in frame will break the spell.

5. **The googly eyes must be practical, cheap, and visible (scene 110).** When Evelyn sticks one on her forehead, it must look like a 99-cent craft supply. Do not make it beautiful or cinematic. It is the ugliest, silliest weapon in the film, and that is the point.
