# Production Design: Designer Pass

## Material World
The film's physical language is a single, overwhelming texture of cheap consumer plastic, office-grade beige, and domestic clutter that refuses to stay in its universe. Every surface—the dining room table buried under tax receipts (scene 2), the cubicle walls covered in outdated posters and "Auditor of the Month" trophies (scene 16), the laundry RV racks stuffed with anonymous clothing bags (scene 13)—feels like it has been accumulated without intention, then weaponized. The palette is aggressively beige, gray, and fluorescent white, punctuated by the violent red of blinking Bluetooth earpieces (scenes 14, 16, 17) and the hot pink of googly eyes on laundry plastic (scene 6). There is no neutral space: the rock field (scene 94) is the only location without human detritus, and it speaks volumes by being empty. The production design’s primary job is to make the audience feel that every object is both a burden and a potential weapon, that the mundane is always one frame away from becoming the absurd.

## Set Dressing Rules

1. **Every surface in the Wang apartment must tell a story of deferred maintenance and failed self-improvement.** The dining room (scenes 1, 2, 4, 12) should contain workout equipment gathering dust, self-help books with dog-eared pages, an old TV showing security feed, and a rice cooker that is perpetually on. The clutter is not random—it is the physical manifestation of Evelyn’s fractured attention. The security feed monitors (scene 1) should be small, cheap, and integrated into the chaos, not prominently framed.

2. **The IRS building’s 10th, 8th, and 9th floors must be functionally identical in their base architecture but differentiated by their state of destruction.** The 10th floor cubicle (scenes 15-18, 56) should feel like a pristine bureaucratic nightmare before the fight, with every desk perfectly aligned and every file color-coded. The 8th floor (scene 25) should feel slightly more lived-in, with a bulletin board that has personal items, to make Deirdre’s table-flip (scene 25) feel like a violation of a semi-safe space. The 9th floor executive office (scenes 67, 80, 85, 124) should be the only location with genuine wood and leather, making the panic room’s contents (scene 80) feel like a secret rot at the heart of the institution.

3. **Every multiverse jump must leave a trace on the base reality.** When Evelyn returns from the Hot Dog universe (scene 62), a single hot-dog finger should briefly remain before snapping back. When Jobu warps reality in the closet universe (scene 18), post-it notes change colors and family portraits change members—these should be practical set changes, not just VFX, so the actors can react to them. The set dressing crew should have a system for rapid, reversible adjustments to wall art and desk items during fight choreography.

4. **The laundromat (scenes 3, 6, 62) should feel like a space that has been optimized for maximum visual noise without being intentional.** The arcade cabinet (scene 3) should have a broken joystick. The TV playing a musical (scene 6) should have a constant static flicker on one side of the screen. The tumbling laundry machines (scene 3) should be practical and loaded with actual clothing, preferably brightly colored items that create motion blur when Joy stares into them. The breakaway front window glass (scene 6, per set requirements) should be a specific type of safety glass that shatters into large, non-lethal shards for Gong Gong’s entrance.

5. **The Empress Hall (scene 91) must be the only location that feels designed, and that design must feel alien.** The white veils should be heavy, practical fabric that requires wind machines to move, not gauzy digital wisps. The space should be completely empty of any other set dressing—no chairs, no tables, no props except the children’s book (scene 91). This emptiness should make the Bagel feel like the only real object in the room, a void that has consumed all context.

6. **The laundry RV’s Alphaverse control room (scene 23) must clash with its mundane exterior.** The exterior should be a standard, beat-up white RV with a faded "Wang's Wash & Fold" decal. The interior should be a rat’s nest of wires, cables, and monitors that feel like they were installed by someone with no budget but immense desperation. The shrine to Alpha Evelyn (scene 23) should use the same kind of cheap candle, plastic flowers, and framed portrait that a grandmother would use, not a military memorial. This keeps the technological invasion grounded in domestic grief.

## Hero Objects

**Googly Eyes** (scenes 6, 110)
- **Narrative job**: Thematic anchor for radical kindness, transforming objects and bullets from weapons into toys.
- **Visual handling**: Must be the cheap, self-adhesive kind with a white plastic base and a black dot that wiggles. In scene 6, they are stuck on laundry plastic bags—random, almost invisible in the clutter. In scene 110, Evelyn sticks one on her forehead like a third eye. This is a prosthetics application: a single large googly eye (approximately 2-inch diameter) with a skin-safe adhesive backing that can be applied and removed multiple times during the fight sequence. When bullets transform into googly eyes (scene 110), the VFX should match the exact material quality of the practical prop—slightly uneven black dot, cheap plastic housing, visible adhesive residue.
- **Multiples/practical notes**: The production needs at least 50 identical googly eyes of various sizes (1-inch for laundry, 2-inch for forehead, smaller for bullet transformations). The bullet-to-googly-eye transformation is a VFX shot, but the aftermath should scatter practical googly eyes on the floor that actors can step on and react to.

**The Bagel** (scenes 67, 91, 118)
- **Narrative job**: Physical manifestation of nihilism, a swirling black hole that is also a breakfast item.
- **Visual handling**: In scene 67, it appears inside Jobu’s palms like a hand-vagina trick—this should be a practical puppet element with a small, glowing CGI addition. In scene 91, it is a giant practical/CGI black hole behind white veils, requiring wind machines for cloth movement and practical lighting that casts shadows on the actors. In scene 118, it physically pulls characters toward it—this is a wire rig and wind machine sequence. The Bagel’s surface should never look like a standard CGI black hole; it should have the texture of a sesame seed bagel that has been spun into a vortex, with visible char marks and seed patterns that spiral inward.
- **Multiples/practical notes**: At least three versions: a small practical puppet (scene 67), a large-scale practical set piece with fiber-optic lighting for the background (scene 91), and a full VFX reference plate with a practical spinning disk for the foreground (scene 118).

**Fanny Pack** (scenes 14, 17)
- **Narrative job**: Comedic weapon, suburban dad accessory turned martial arts tool.
- **Visual handling**: Must look like something Waymond would buy at a department store—nylon, black or navy blue, with a visible brand logo that is slightly too large. The zippers should be functional but reinforced with hidden steel cables. The belt loops should be padded internally to prevent chafing during the fast-spinning choreography (scene 17). The interior should be weighted with a sewn-in lead sheet or sandbag so it carries momentum like a flail.
- **Multiples/practical notes**: At least four identical fanny packs: one for rehearsal (stunt padding, no weight), one for close-ups (full weight, visible wear), one for full-contact choreography (full weight, reinforced stitching, no logo for continuity), and one backup. Each should have a functional main compartment that can hold the divorce papers (scene 14).

**Auditor of the Month Trophy** (scenes 16, 80)
- **Narrative job**: Symbol of bureaucratic absurdity, used as a weapon and a jump pad.
- **Visual handling**: Must be unequivocally phallic—a gold-plated, abstract shape on a wooden base that looks like a butt plug mounted on a plaque. The inscription should read "Auditor of the Month" in a generic serif font. The base should be heavy enough to be used as a bludgeon, but the gold top should be hollow to shatter on impact (scene 80). When used as a jump pad (scene 16), the gold top should come off easily to reveal a hidden spring mechanism that the stunt team can compress.
- **Multiples/practical notes**: Three versions: a pristine hero trophy (gold intact, plaque pristine), a battle-damaged version (gold chipped, plaque cracked), and a breakaway version (hollow gold shell filled with plaster dust for the shattering moment in scene 80).

**Divorce Papers** (scenes 2, 14, 17, 118)
- **Narrative job**: Waymond’s hidden pain, later repurposed as verse-jumping instructions.
- **Visual handling**: The papers should look like standard legal documents—cream-colored, typed, with Waymond’s signature on the last page (practical, not graphic). In scene 2, they are hidden under tax receipts, slightly crumpled. In scene 14, Evelyn pulls them out and reads them—the lighting on the paper should be practical (overhead fluorescent) so the audience can see the text. In scene 17, the back of the papers has handwritten instructions from Alpha Waymond in blue ballpoint pen. In scene 118, the papers are crumpled and discarded on the floor—a small but visible character beat.
- **Multiples/practical notes**: Five identical sets of papers with the same text. One set with the back-side instructions already written for the close-up in scene 17. All papers should have a slight coffee stain on the corner (Waymond’s nervous habit) to make them feel lived-in.

**Baseball Bat** (scenes 6, 110)
- **Narrative job**: Evelyn’s weapon of generational frustration, later repurposed with a googly eye.
- **Visual handling**: In scene 6, it is a standard aluminum bat that Evelyn uses to thump the air vent—the sound should be a sharp, hollow metal clang. In scene 110, the bat has a googly eye stuck to the barrel, turning it into a silly but effective weapon. The bat should have visible wear: scuffs from the vent (scene 6), a small dent in the barrel, and tape residue on the handle where Evelyn has regripped it.
- **Multiples/practical notes**: Three bats: one pristine for early scenes (no googly eye), one with a pre-applied googly eye for scene 110, and one stunt bat made of a breakaway resin for any impact with actors.

## Wardrobe Logic

**Evelyn Wang’s arc is told through her refusal to change clothes.** She begins the film in an oversized t-shirt and thin outdated designer glasses (scene 2)—a woman who has given up on presentation. Her 15 wardrobe changes across the multiverse are not a montage of glamour; they are a series of increasingly absurd uniforms that she is forced into (Movie Star gown in scene 48, dominantrix leather in scene 110’s flash). The moment she chooses to stay in her base-reality clothes while verse-jumping (around scene 90) signals her integration of the multiverse into her mundane self. The final shot (scene 124) shows her holding Waymond’s hand in the same oversized t-shirt, but now it looks like a choice, not a surrender.

**Waymond’s wardrobe is a study in deliberate blandness that hides capability.** His ill-fitting polo shirt and transition-lens glasses (scene 2) make him look like a man who has given up on trying. Alpha Waymond’s costume (scenes 14, 16, 17) should be the exact same polo shirt but tucked in, sleeves rolled, and glasses removed—a small adjustment that reads as a different man. The fanny pack (scene 14) is the only accessory that crosses universes, grounding his combat prowess in suburban accessory.

**Jobu Tupaki’s 20 costume changes are not fashion—they are a scream for attention.** Each outfit should be a complete persona: Elvis jumpsuit (scene 56) with visible sweat stains, alien royalty (scene 91) with impractical shoulder pads that she has to navigate doorways in, a blood-splattered bib (scene 18) that she wears like a celebrity at a lobster restaurant. The costumes should look like they were pulled from a costume shop bin and worn with aggressive indifference—not couture. The only constant is the sharpie circle on her followers’ foreheads (scene 18, 25, 56, 91)—a makeshift uniformity that is as lazy as it is menacing.

**Deirdre’s cheap suit (scene 15) is the armor of bureaucratic desperation.** It should be slightly too shiny, slightly too tight, with a visible stain on the lapel that never goes away. Her hand grip strengthener (scene 15) is a character detail that suggests constant, unnoticed tension. In the Hot Dog universe (scene 62, 107), her wardrobe should be identical—a joke that even in a universe without thumbs, she still wears the same cheap suit.

**Gong Gong’s white undershirt and slippers (scene 6) are a deliberate refusal to perform for the world.** He is the only character who does not change costumes when verse-jumping—Alpha Gong Gong (scenes 67, 85, 101, 110) wears the same undershirt and slippers, but his wheelchair is rigged with office supplies. The body is the same; the context is weaponized.

## Motif Continuity

**Red vs. Beige**: The color red appears exclusively on electronic devices (Bluetooth earpieces blinking red in scenes 14, 16, 17, 25, 80, 110), as a warning or activation signal. Beige is the color of bureaucracy and domesticity—the cubicle walls, the apartment walls, the tax receipts, Deirdre’s suit. When Jobu appears, she introduces color like a contaminant: the Elvis jumpsuit’s white and gold, the alien royalty’s deep purple, the sharpie black on foreheads. The Bagel is the absence of all color—a dark void that absorbs light.

**Circles and Holes**: The googly eye is a circle with a black dot. The Bagel is a circle with a hole. The sharpie circle on foreheads is a circle with no dot. This shape language repeats across universes—the round mirror in the karaoke scene (scene 1), the tumbling laundry machine door (scene 3), the Auditor of the Month trophy’s phallic curve (scene 16), the donut shop sign in the background of the laundromat (scene 3). The design team should ensure that every location has at least one circular object that can be framed as a visual echo.

**Duplication and Clutter**: Every set should feel like it has more objects than it needs. The apartment (scenes 1, 2, 4, 12) has multiple TVs, a security feed monitor, a rice cooker, a microwave, and a karaoke machine in one room. The laundromat (scene 3) has washing machines, dryers, an arcade cabinet, a TV, and laundry carts. The IRS cubicles (scenes 15-18, 56) have identical desks with identical accessories. This duplication creates a visual anxiety that mirrors Evelyn’s overwhelmed mind—there is always another object to process.

**The Glow of Screens**: Practical light sources come from screens: the security feed monitors (scene 1), the tumbling laundry machine’s internal light (scene 3), the RV’s monitor arrays (scene 13, 23), the elevator’s security camera light (scene 14), the cubicle computer monitors (scene 15). These are the only warm light sources in a world of cold fluorescent overheads. When characters are about to verse-jump, their Bluetooth earpieces glow red (scene 14), a small screen-like light that signals the interface between universes.

**Transformed Objects**: The film’s action language is built on turning mundane objects into weapons: baseball bat (scene 6), fanny pack (scene 17), trophy (scene 80), riot shield (scene 80), spatulas (scene 80), belt whip (scene 110), office-supply robot exoskeleton (scene 110). Each object must look like its real-world counterpart until the moment it is wielded. The production design team should have a catalog of every object used as a weapon and ensure that the props department has at least two versions of each: one that looks completely normal and one that is reinforced for stunts.

## What To Avoid

- **Do not make the multiverse spaces look clean or designed.** Every alternate universe should feel like it was decorated by someone with the same budget and taste as the base universe. The hot-dog universe (scene 62) is the same laundromat. The rock field (scene 94) is a dirt patch. The Empress Hall (scene 91) is empty. Avoid any set that looks like a sci-fi television show’s idea of another dimension.

- **Do not treat the googly eyes as a mascot or a joke that has worn thin.** They are small, cheap, and almost invisible in early scenes. In scene 6, they are background clutter. Only in scene 110 do they become front-and-center. The design team must resist the urge to put googly eyes on everything in early scenes to set them up—they should be almost missable.

- **Do not glamorize the Evelyn/Jobu fights.** The Viking fight (scene 90) should look like a community theater production—costumes that are clearly fake, weapons that are clearly foam. The piñata fight (scene 90) should look like a child’s birthday party gone wrong. The climax (scene 118) should feel exhausting and messy, not choreographed and clean. The production design should support the actors’ fatigue, not hide it.

- **Do not let the IRS building feel like a real corporate office.** It should feel slightly off—the fluorescent lights too bright, the cubicle walls a color of beige that does not exist in nature, the carpets patternless yet offensive. The “Auditor of the Month” trophy (scene 16) is a sign that this building has been self-important for too long. Everything should feel like it was purchased from a liquidation sale in 1998 and never replaced.

- **Do not use the Bagel as a mere visual effect.** It is a practical set piece with a VFX enhancement. The white veils in Empress Hall (scene 91) must be real fabric that the actors can push through and react to. The wind from the Bagel must be a practical wind machine that affects hair and clothing, not just a digital particle effect. When Evelyn pulls Joy from the Bagel (scene 118), they should both be visibly clinging to something physical—a wire rig, a handhold, anything that sells the weight of the pull.

- **Do not forget that the rock universe (scene 94) is a production design challenge in restraint.** Two rocks on a dirt field. No props. No wardrobe. No VFX except subtitles. The rocks must be selected for their natural shapes that suggest eyes and a mouth without being literal—they are actors in their own right. The field must be empty of any human sign—no power lines, no tire tracks, no litter. This is the only set where the design team should actively remove things rather than add them.
