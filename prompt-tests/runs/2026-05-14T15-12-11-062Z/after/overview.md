# Overview: Everything Everywhere All At Once

## The Film In One Breath

A laundromat owner drowning in tax receipts, hidden divorce papers, overcooked noodles, and family avoidance is forced to see every possible version of her life, then choose the one in front of her: the IRS office, the wounded husband, the daughter she failed to name honestly, and the family that finally links arms to pull Joy back from the Bagel in scene 118.

## Why This Film

The film works because the cosmic scale is always tied to a production object or room Evelyn already knows. Scene 2 gives us the real pressure: tax documents, receipts, a rice cooker, hidden divorce papers, and Evelyn stuck at the apartment table. Scene 14 turns that private pressure into genre mechanics when the compact umbrella blocks the elevator camera, the Bluetooth earpieces blink red, and the divorce papers become verse-jump material. Scene 17 proves the tone: Alpha Waymond eats chapstick and fights security with a reinforced fanny pack, but Evelyn has just found the divorce papers, so the action is also a marriage scene.

Joy’s story needs the same discipline. Scene 3 introduces her as absence, staring into the tumbling washer until Becky kisses her back into the room. Scene 6 makes the wound public when Evelyn introduces Becky as Joy’s “good friend” in the laundromat, surrounded by googly eyes on laundry plastic, Gong Gong’s cane, customers, and the baseball bat Evelyn uses to summon Waymond. By scene 91, Joy-as-Jobu reveals the Bagel behind white veils in the Empress Hall; by scene 94, the same crisis becomes two rocks in an empty field; by scene 118, the answer is physical, with Evelyn, Waymond, and Gong Gong linking arms against wind and darkness.

The promise is not multiverse spectacle for its own sake. It is a family drama where every absurd image has a job: the Elvis jumpsuit and pig in scene 56 make Jobu’s threat feel theatrical and unstable; the hot dog fingers in scene 62 turn humiliation into intimacy; Waymond’s bleeding plea for kindness in scene 101 changes the fight language; the googly-eye third eye and transformed bullets in scene 110 make Evelyn’s new strategy visible.

## Scope Read

This is a 125-page contemporary film with 28 listed scenes, but the actual production load is heavier than the count suggests because many scenes contain nested universes, rapid montage, split-screen action, or simultaneous emotional resolutions.

The location pattern is controlled but intense. The core build is interior-heavy: apartment dining/living room scenes 1, 2, 4, and 12; laundromat scenes 3, 6, and 62; laundry RV scenes 13 and 23; then a large IRS block spanning elevator, cubicles, closet, 8th floor, 9th floor office, panic room, and atrium across scenes 14 through 18, 25, 67, 80, 85, 86, 101, 110, and 124. The main exterior load is light on paper, with the rock field in scene 94, but “various universes” in scenes 90, 107, and 118 will behave like multiple mini-units unless tightly designed.

Night work appears limited: scene 1 is apartment night with karaoke, disco lights, and the round mirror. Most remaining listed work is day interior or continuous interior. The schedule pressure is less about night/exterior logistics and more about dense interior resets, breakaway walls, stunt safety, practical effects, wardrobe changes, and VFX continuity.

Cast pressure is centered on Evelyn. She appears in nearly every key block, carries martial arts, opera, sign spinning, hot dog finger work, emotional family scenes, and 15 wardrobe changes. Waymond requires both soft domestic presence and fanny-pack fight precision, with Alpha Waymond adding separate tactical behavior in scenes 14, 16, 17, 23, 25, and 86. Jobu is a major wardrobe/makeup engine: 20 wardrobe changes, possession logic, stunts, a pig handler in scene 56, and major VFX interaction with the Bagel in scenes 67, 91, and 118.

The VFX/stunt burden is high. Required work includes the life-path montage in scene 14, split screen and neck break in scene 16, fanny-pack martial arts in scene 17, reality-warping and absurd death montage in scene 18, pro-wrestling Deirdre in scene 25, slow-motion martial arts and drywall impact in scene 48, confetti transformation and salsa/human-shield gag in scene 56, hot dog prosthetics/VFX in scene 62, CGI Bagel reveal in scene 67, sign-spinning combat and dog-as-weapon choreography in scene 80, pinky-finger wirework in scene 85, rapid universe switching in scene 90, giant Bagel winds in scene 91, subtitle-only rock staging in scene 94, googly-eye bullet transformations and mech suit action in scene 110, and 10-plus universe resolution cutting in scene 118.

## Hidden Complexity

- The IRS is not one set. Scenes 14 through 18, 25, 67, 80, 85, 86, 101, 110, and 124 require elevator security cameras, cubicle walls, a janitor closet with breakaway door, barricade-ready filing cabinets, a secret bookshelf door, a panic room with vents, an atrium with crushable banisters, and enough continuity to make the office feel like one building.

- Hero props need stunt, VFX, and emotional continuity. The divorce papers appear in scenes 2, 14, 17, and 118; the fanny pack must read as ordinary in scene 14 and perform as a reinforced weapon in scene 17; Bluetooth earpieces need practical red, green, and yellow LED states across scenes 14, 16, 17, 25, 80, and 110.

- Jobu is a schedule multiplier. Scene 56 alone includes an Elvis jumpsuit, pig on leash, cigarette, ketchup bottle, transformed baton, confetti guard effect, salsa/human-shield action, and autonomous photo-frame changes. Scenes 91 and 118 add alien royalty, white veils, Bagel winds, and the final pull.

- The comedy requires serious physical planning. Scene 25 has papercuts while running, a super-powered table flip, backbreaker, and drop kick; scene 80 combines gas masks, opera breath, riot-shield sign spinning, pipe drumming, spatulas, trophies, and a French Bulldog weapon gag; scene 110 adds belt whip, ball gag transformation, shoulder riding, and Alpha Gong Gong’s office-supply mech.

- The quiet scenes are not throwaways. Scene 94 is only two rocks, wind, subtitles, dirt field, and a cliff edge, but it must land after the Bagel reveal in scene 91 and before the family pull in scene 118. Its simplicity needs protected time, not leftover coverage.

## Demo-Worthy Hook

The front-page sell for a creative team is the object chain: the film can move from a round karaoke mirror in scene 1 to receipts and hidden divorce papers in scene 2, a tumbling washer and wounded daughter in scene 3, a fanny-pack fight in scene 17, Jobu with a pig and Elvis jumpsuit in scene 56, the white-veiled Bagel in scene 91, two silent rocks in scene 94, and a family linking arms in scene 118 without changing what the movie is about. That is the lean-forward opportunity: every department gets to make something strange, but the strange thing always points back to Evelyn choosing Joy, Waymond, Gong Gong, the audit, and the room she tried to escape.
