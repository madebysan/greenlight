# Title & Palette: Graphic Design Pass

## Title Read

The title should feel crowded first, then chosen.

Typographically, "Everything Everywhere All At Once" should not sit like a clean cosmic statement. It should behave like Evelyn's table in scene 2: too many tax documents, receipts, a calculator, a rice cooker, and hidden divorce papers pressing into the same domestic space. The title can stack, interrupt, repeat, or slightly misregister, but it should still resolve into one readable block. That matters because the film keeps asking the same question in different forms: the tumbling laundry machine Joy stares into in scene 3, the Bagel Jobu reveals in scene 67, the two rocks in scene 94, and the family linking arms in scene 118.

The strongest title move is a dense, practical word shape with one circular breach. A round counter, dot, "O", or custom ring can carry the mirror from scene 1, laundry motion from scene 3, the googly eyes from scenes 6 and 110, and the Bagel from scenes 67 and 91. The circle should not feel ornamental. It is the film's argument: attention can become a trap, a joke, a weapon, or a way back to Joy.

Keep the lettering more domestic than celestial. Receipts, office forms, sticky notes, laundry signage, and IRS cubicles are better references than stars or galaxies. The multiverse should enter as pressure on the typography, not as a space background.

## Palette System

- `#F3E8D2` Receipt paper  
  Use as the main warm neutral. It comes from the years of tax documents and receipts covering Evelyn's apartment table in scene 2, then returning inside the IRS audit in scene 15.

- `#2B2B26` Bagel black  
  The deep anchor color. It belongs to Jobu opening her hands to reveal the churning black hole Bagel in scene 67 and the darkness pulling her in during scene 118.

- `#F7F2E8` Veil white  
  A blown, almost ceremonial white from the empress hall in scene 91, where massive white veils hide the Bagel. Use it sparingly so it feels like exposure, not cleanliness.

- `#D0342C` IRS red note  
  The sharp administrative accent. Pull it from the red sticky notes in scene 124 and the private wound of divorce papers surfacing across scenes 2, 14, 17, and 118.

- `#F2C94C` Googly eye yellow  
  A small absurd highlight for buttons, rings, or title apertures. Tie it to the googly eyes on laundry plastic in scene 6 and Evelyn's third-eye gesture in scene 110.

- `#4A7C59` Earpiece green  
  A signal color for active states, route lines, and deck navigation. It comes from the bluetooth earpieces that require red, green, and yellow LED lights across scenes 14, 16, 17, 25, 80, and 110.

- `#6D7F8F` Laundromat steel  
  A secondary cool gray-blue from tumbling machines, laundry carts, old arcade cabinet, and the wash-and-fold environment in scenes 3 and 6. Use it to keep the system grounded before the cosmic black enters.

## Typography Direction

Use a condensed display face for the title, but do not make it slick. It should have enough pressure to feel like too much information in one place, like the apartment dining room in scenes 1, 2, 4, and 12: workout equipment, self-help books, old TV, security feed, rice cooker, microwave, family photos, receipts.

Set the title in stacked blocks with uneven line lengths:

EVERYTHING  
EVERYWHERE  
ALL AT ONCE

Let one circular form interrupt the stack. It can replace an "O", sit behind the words, or punch through the lockup. That circle should be able to read as the mirror in scene 1, the laundry machine in scene 3, the Bagel in scenes 67 and 91, or the googly eye in scene 110 depending on the slide.

Secondary type should be plain and useful: a bureaucratic sans or mono-adjacent face that can carry scene numbers, slug lines, prop labels, and palette notes. The IRS material in scenes 15 through 18 needs the deck to understand forms, tabs, labels, and file systems. Do not over-style the supporting text.

Spacing should alternate between compression and relief. Crowded title slides can echo scene 2's receipt table or scene 17's cubicle fight. Quiet slides need the restraint of scene 94: two rocks, empty dirt field, text on screen, no decoration fighting the pause.

## Deck Identity Rules

Use circles as a system, not a motif pasted everywhere. A circle can be a mask, crop, bullet, cursor, title counter, loading state, or palette chip. It should connect scene 1's round mirror, scene 3's tumbling machine, scene 67's Bagel, scene 91's veiled reveal, and scene 110's googly-eye third eye.

Let paperwork structure the deck. Section dividers can borrow from tax forms, receipts, audit stamps, sticky notes, file labels, and office grid lines because Evelyn's pressure begins with documents in scene 2 and returns with Deirdre's audit in scene 15. Keep the forms slightly off, overfilled, or interrupted so they never become corporate-clean.

Keep the cosmic effects physically tethered. If a slide uses black void, it should touch a receipt edge, a laundry machine circle, a cubicle wall, a fanny pack path from scene 17, or the family pull in scene 118. The film's scale works because the Bagel and the IRS office live in the same emotional system.

Use absurd objects as evidence markers. The compact umbrella covering the elevator camera in scene 14, the fanny pack in scene 17, the Auditor trophy in scenes 16 and 80, the riot shield sign-spin in scene 80, and the baseball bat with a googly eye in scene 110 can become small iconography or slide punctuation.

Protect the Mood & Tone tab by making it the most precise section visually. It should move between cluttered domestic pressure, IRS combat geometry, white Bagel ceremony, rock-field stillness, and the linked-arm pull. Those are not moods in the abstract. They are scene-backed states.

## Thumbnail Read

At small size, the system should reduce to three things: a cramped title block, one circular interruption, and a paper-black-yellow palette hit.

The thumbnail should still suggest the film if all detail drops out. The readable ingredients are the title stack, the Bagel or googly-eye circle, and the receipt-paper field. That holds because the screenplay repeats circles across scene 1's mirror, scene 3's washer, scenes 67 and 91's Bagel, and scene 110's googly eye.

Avoid thumbnail detail that depends on tiny props. The fanny pack from scene 17, sticky notes from scene 124, or divorce papers from scene 118 are better as supporting texture in full-size slides. At thumbnail scale, the circle must carry the idea.

## Avoid

1. Do not make the title look like generic space sci-fi. The script's pressure begins with tax documents, receipts, noodles, laundry, and hidden divorce papers in scenes 2, 3, 4, and 6.

2. Do not use a clean luxury A24-style title treatment that erases the mess. Evelyn's apartment is overpopulated with equipment, books, monitors, rice cooker, microwave, and paperwork in scenes 1, 2, 4, and 12.

3. Do not turn the Bagel into a decorative portal. In scene 67 it is Jobu's nihilistic lure, in scene 91 it is revealed behind white veils, and in scene 118 Joy is physically pulled back from it.

4. Do not overuse googly eyes as cute branding. They matter because they shift from laundry plastic in scene 6 to Evelyn's forehead and harmless bullets in scene 110.

5. Do not smooth out the family wound. The divorce papers in scenes 2, 14, 17, and 118, Joy's washing-machine stare in scene 3, the misintroduction of Becky in scene 6, and the linked-arm pull in scene 118 should stay present in the design logic.
