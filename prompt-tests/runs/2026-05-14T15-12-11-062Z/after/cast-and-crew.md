# Cast & Crew: Casting Pass

## Casting Thesis

The ensemble has to make the film feel domestic even when the action becomes cosmic. Evelyn’s performer carries the heaviest load: she starts boxed in by receipts and tax documents at the apartment table in scene 2, deflects Joy’s hurt through overcooked noodles and Becky’s pronouns in scene 4, absorbs the divorce papers and fanny-pack fight in scene 17, then has to make the silent rock field in scene 94 and the family arm-link pull in scene 118 feel like the same emotional argument. Cast for actors who can let absurd objects stay emotionally active: a compact umbrella in scene 14, hot dog fingers in scene 62, the Bagel in scenes 67 and 91, and googly eyes in scene 110.

## Principal Roles

### Evelyn Wang

Casting energy: exhausted, fast-thinking, emotionally overbooked, with a body that can look both practical and suddenly mythic. She cannot read as “secret superhero” too early; scene 2 needs a woman drowning in receipts, clutter, and marital static.

Performance burden: Evelyn has to track multiple forms of overload without turning them into one mood. Scene 4 asks for avoidance through cooking and pronoun mistakes. Scene 15 asks for bureaucratic dread while she secretly switches shoes under the desk. Scene 48 asks for panic turning into a successful kung fu jump. Scene 101 asks her to receive Waymond’s bleeding plea for kindness without making it sentimental. Scene 124 asks for serenity while still being momentarily distracted by everything.

Relationship tension: with Joy, the burden is denial, recognition, and finally choice. The wound begins with Joy staring into the washer in scene 3 and Evelyn introducing Becky as a “good friend” in scene 6, then comes back through Jobu’s Bagel temptation in scenes 67 and 91, the rock conversation in scene 94, and the physical pull in scene 118.

Physical/special demands: extensive martial arts, sign spinning, opera singing, hot dog finger prosthetics, 15 wardrobe changes, and a performance that can hold stillness as strongly as action.

### Waymond Wang / Alpha Waymond

Casting energy: gentle enough that the divorce papers in scenes 2, 14, 17, and 118 hurt, but precise enough that the scene 14 umbrella shift can become tactical in one beat. The actor needs two centers of gravity: ordinary Waymond’s softness and Alpha Waymond’s mission focus.

Performance burden: ordinary Waymond must never feel weak just because other characters underestimate him. His kindness has to earn scene 101, where he is bleeding after being stabbed and still asks everyone to be kind in the IRS battlefield. Alpha Waymond has to sell the rules quickly through the elevator and closet material in scenes 14 and 16, then become physically credible in scene 17 when the fanny pack becomes a combat weapon.

Relationship tension: with Evelyn, the chemistry is a marriage where tenderness has been buried under chores, receipts, and avoidance. Scene 1 gives a hazy karaoke memory of family sweetness. Scene 2 gives hidden divorce intent. Scene 86 gives the interrupted almost-kiss as Alpha Waymond’s body goes limp. Scene 124 needs the handhold to feel chosen, not reset.

Physical/special demands: fanny-pack martial arts choreography, stunt precision, and clear differentiation between Waymond’s ill-fitting polo softness and Alpha Waymond’s tactical urgency.

### Joy Wang / Jobu Tupaki

Casting energy: the role needs an actor who can make absence legible before spectacle arrives. Joy’s blank stare into the tumbling washer in scene 3 and her hurt after being reduced to Becky’s “good friend” in scene 6 are the base note. Jobu’s Elvis jumpsuit with a pig in scene 56 and alien royalty entrance in scene 91 only work if they are extensions of that pain.

Performance burden: Joy has to carry the private ache of wanting her mother to see her. Jobu has to carry omniversal overload without playing chaos as a pose. Scene 18 introduces her through small boots, a hostage game, and lethal tickling. Scene 67 puts the Bagel in her hands as a philosophy. Scene 94 strips everything down to two rocks and text. Scene 118 asks the same performer to be both the threat and the person being pulled back.

Relationship tension: with Evelyn, this is the central casting test. The actor must be able to oppose Evelyn at maximum scale in scenes 67, 91, and 110, then still feel like the daughter from scenes 3, 4, and 6 when the family links arms in scene 118.

Physical/special demands: rapid wardrobe and makeup changes, stunts, 20 wardrobe changes, pig handler coordination, and the ability to move between deadpan stillness, threat, comedy, and despair.

### Gong Gong / Alpha Gong Gong

Casting energy: ordinary Gong Gong should feel like inherited judgment sitting in the room. Alpha Gong Gong should feel like that same judgment militarized. The cane, slippers, and wheelchair matter because the pressure is familial before it is tactical.

Performance burden: scene 6 requires the actor’s presence to make Evelyn’s shame visible when Becky is misintroduced. Scenes 12 through 16 keep him close to Evelyn and Waymond during the family’s IRS pressure. Alpha Gong Gong in scenes 67, 85, 101, and 110 must be severe enough to make his assassination logic credible without becoming a flat villain.

Relationship tension: with Evelyn, he is the parent she still performs for. His presence raises the cost of accepting Joy in scene 6, then becomes part of the final tether in scene 118 when he joins the family pull.

Physical/special demands: wheelchair work, wheelchair stunts, and a makeshift office-supply robot exoskeleton for Alpha Gong Gong.

### Deirdre Atkins / Deirdre (Bagel)

Casting energy: cast someone who can make bureaucracy physically comic without making Deirdre stupid. The cheap suit and hand grip strengthener in scene 15 should feel like armor. Her antagonism is rooted in procedure before the multiverse turns her into a combat obstacle.

Performance burden: Deirdre has to pivot from audit pressure in scenes 15 through 17 to possessed threat in scene 16, physical attacker in scene 48, hot dog universe partner in scene 62, and recipient of Evelyn’s empathy in scene 107. The actor needs sharp timing: the comedy comes from absolute seriousness inside absurd situations.

Relationship tension: with Evelyn, Deirdre is first the face of the IRS battlefield. By scene 107, Evelyn stopping Deirdre’s blade with her foot while wiping her tears with toes in another universe turns the casting need into vulnerability under hostility.

Physical/special demands: pro wrestling stunts, hot dog finger prosthetics, bagel-zealot physicality, and stunt work for Deirdre (Bagel).

### Becky Sregor

Casting energy: grounded, watchful, and secure enough to make the family’s avoidance visible. Becky’s tattoos and septum piercing should not become shorthand; her job is to show what Joy has outside the Wang family.

Performance burden: Becky has limited scenes, so each beat has to clarify Joy’s stakes. In scene 3, she snaps Joy out of the washer stare with a kiss. In scene 4, Evelyn avoids her while dumping soy sauce and scallions into overcooked noodles. In scene 6, the “good friend” introduction lands because Becky is present as the person being erased.

Relationship tension: with Joy, she is support. With Evelyn, she is the truth Evelyn keeps trying to soften for Gong Gong. Cast for someone who can hold discomfort without stealing focus.

Physical/special demands: no special physical requirements listed, but the actor needs precise reactive work in scenes 3, 4, and 6.

## Chemistry Map

Evelyn and Joy are the film’s main pressure point. Their chemistry has to contain the washer silence in scene 3, the “good friend” wound in scene 6, Jobu’s Bagel reveal in scene 67, the rock-universe pause in scene 94, and the family pull in scene 118. If the casting only works in confrontation, the ending will feel mechanical. If it only works in tenderness, Jobu will lose danger.

Evelyn and Waymond need lived-in marital fatigue. Scene 2 has receipts, clutter, and hidden divorce intent. Scene 17 brings the divorce papers into a fight where Alpha Waymond is suddenly untouchable. Scene 101 reframes Waymond’s softness as strategy. Scene 124 needs the handhold to feel like Evelyn choosing this ordinary life after everything else.

Joy and Becky need quiet credibility. Scene 3 gives them the kiss by the washing machine. Scene 6 shows how fragile that safety becomes under Evelyn and Gong Gong’s gaze. Becky should make Joy’s hurt clearer, not announce it.

Evelyn and Deirdre need comic hostility that can turn into intimacy. The audit desk in scene 15, the stairwell attack in scene 48, the hot dog fingers in scene 62, and the foot-and-toe tenderness in scene 107 all depend on actors willing to treat humiliation and desire seriously.

Waymond and Gong Gong are opposing answers to crisis. Alpha Gong Gong brings assassination logic in scenes 67, 85, 101, and 110. Waymond, bleeding in scene 101, argues for kindness in the same battlefield. Cast them so Evelyn is visibly pulled between inherited judgment and chosen mercy.

## Specialty Department Flags

- Stunt coordination: fanny-pack combat in scene 17, Deirdre’s pro-wrestling physicality, Alpha Waymond’s papercut chase in scene 25, riot-shield sign-spinning in scene 80, pinky-finger attacks in scene 85, and multiverse fighting in scene 90.

- Movement coaching: Evelyn’s physical vocabulary has to shift from shoe-switching compliance in scene 15 to kung fu in scene 48, opera-breath action in scene 80, rock stillness in scene 94, and empathy-based combat in scene 110.

- Prosthetics and specialty makeup: hot dog fingers for Evelyn and Deirdre in scenes 62 and 107, Jobu’s rapid costume and makeup changes across scenes 56, 67, 91, 101, 110, and the sharpie-circle Bagel look for Deirdre in scenes 18, 25, 56, 91, and 107.

- Animal handling: Jobu’s pig on a leash in scene 56 needs safe coordination because the moment is both threat introduction and character reveal.

- Casting/stunt doubling integration: Evelyn, Waymond/Alpha Waymond, Jobu, Deirdre, and Alpha Gong Gong all need doubles or movement support that preserve character behavior, especially in scenes 17, 48, 80, 85, 90, 101, and 110.

- Voice and music support: Evelyn’s opera universe in scene 80 and the karaoke family image in scene 1 need performers or coaching that can make singing feel character-based, not decorative.

## What Not To Cast

- Do not cast Evelyn as a polished action lead from the start; scene 2 needs the receipts, clutter, gray hair, outdated glasses, and exhaustion to feel real before scene 48 works.

- Do not cast Waymond as merely goofy or weak; scene 101 only lands if his kindness has been active since the divorce papers and fanny-pack contrast in scene 17.

- Do not cast Jobu as random chaos; the Elvis jumpsuit and pig in scene 56, Bagel in scene 67, veil reveal in scene 91, and rock silence in scene 94 all have to come from Joy’s overload.

- Do not cast Gong Gong as a simple scold; his pressure in scene 6 and his participation in the arm-link pull in scene 118 both need emotional weight.

- Do not cast Deirdre as a cartoon bureaucrat; the hand grip in scene 15, hot dog-finger tenderness in scene 107, and final IRS-office presence in scene 124 need a person under the antagonism.
