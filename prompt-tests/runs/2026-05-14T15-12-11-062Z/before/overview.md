# Everything Everywhere All At Once

## Logline
When an ordinary laundromat owner discovers she's connected to infinite parallel universes, she must master multiversal combat to stop her daughter—now possessed by a nihilistic chaos agent—from destroying all existence.

## Taglines
- Everything breaks. Nothing matters. Choose anyway.
- One woman. Infinite lives. One choice.
- The multiverse has a heartbeat.

## Synopsis
Evelyn Wang is a exhausted 55-year-old Chinese laundromat owner drowning in tax documents, family expectations, and the wreckage of her marriage. When her meek husband Waymond arrives at an IRS audit with a cryptic message, Evelyn discovers she's somehow connected to infinite parallel universes—and that a chaotic omniversal being called Jobu Tupaki, now inhabiting her estranged daughter Joy's body, is systematically destroying realities in search of an Evelyn who shares her nihilistic worldview.

As government agents, interdimensional warriors, and her own father's military forces close in, Evelyn must leap between wildly disparate universes—encountering kung fu masters, sentient hot dogs, and versions of herself who made radically different choices—to master impossible powers and confront the painful truth at the heart of the conflict: her daughter's despair.

The film escalates from intimate family drama into absurdist cosmic warfare, ultimately asking whether love and kindness can survive the knowledge that nothing matters.

## Themes

**Nihilism vs. Existentialism**
The entire conflict hinges on whether meaninglessness demands surrender or whether choosing connection despite futility is the ultimate act of rebellion. Jobu represents the seductive pull of nihilism; Waymond and eventually Evelyn embody existential choice.

**Generational Trauma and Expectations**
Evelyn's relationship with her father mirrors her conflict with Joy—cycles of criticism, disappointment, and unmet expectations that span generations. The climax literally pulls Joy back from the abyss through familial acceptance rather than judgment.

**The Power of Radical Kindness**
Waymond's philosophy—that kindness is a survival strategy—becomes the film's secret weapon. Evelyn's shift from combat to empathy, particularly in her encounter with Deirdre, demonstrates that compassion scales across infinite realities.

**Regret and 'What Ifs'**
The multiverse itself is constructed from unlived lives and abandoned paths. Evelyn confronts versions of herself who pursued different dreams, ultimately accepting her own messy, mundane reality as enough.

**Mother-Daughter Reconciliation**
Joy's existential rejection of her mother masks desperate hunger for acceptance. The film's emotional climax isn't a victory—it's Evelyn, Waymond, and even Gong Gong physically pulling Joy out of the Bagel, choosing her despite everything.
